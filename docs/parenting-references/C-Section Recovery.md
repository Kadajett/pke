## Recovery Timeline

### Week 1 (Hospital + First Days Home)
- **Pain:** Most intense. Managed with prescribed pain medication (typically ibuprofen + acetaminophen alternating; sometimes short-course opioids)
- **Mobility:** Getting out of bed is hard — use the "log roll" technique (roll to side, push up with arms)
- **Incision:** Steri-strips or surgical glue; dressing may be removed 24–48 hours post-op
- **Bleeding:** Lochia (vaginal bleeding) is normal — moderate to heavy, like a period
- **Swelling:** Legs and feet may swell significantly from IV fluids — resolves over days
- **Gas pain:** Very common post-abdominal surgery; walking helps
- **Breastfeeding:** Side-lying or football hold protects the incision

### Weeks 2–3
- **Pain:** Improving but still sore, especially with sudden movements, coughing, laughing
- **Hold a pillow** over incision when coughing/sneezing/laughing — splinting reduces pain
- **Activity:** Short walks around the house; gradually increasing
- **Driving:** NOT recommended yet (most OBs say 2–4 weeks minimum; must be off opioid pain meds and able to brake suddenly)
- **Incision:** Steri-strips may start to fall off — don't pull them; let them go naturally
- **Constipation:** Common — stool softeners, fiber, hydration

### Weeks 4–6
- **Pain:** Mild discomfort; most don't need medication
- **Activity:** Can do light housework; still no heavy lifting (>10 lbs, roughly heavier than the baby)
- **6-week OB checkup** — incision check, cleared for exercise and sex (if desired)
- **Driving:** Usually cleared by now
- **Numbness:** Area around incision may be numb/tingly — nerve regeneration, can take months to a year

### 6 Weeks – 3 Months
- **Exercise:** Gradual return; start with walking, pelvic floor work, gentle core
- **NO crunches/sit-ups** until cleared by provider and pelvic floor is assessed
- **Incision scar:** Pink/red → gradually fading; silicone strips or massage can help
- **Full internal healing:** Uterine incision takes ~6–8 weeks to heal

### 3 – 12 Months
- **Scar maturation:** Continues for up to a year; scar massage (gentle circular motions) after 6 weeks can reduce adhesions
- **Core strength:** May take 6–12 months to feel "normal" — this is expected after major abdominal surgery
- **Diastasis recti:** Check for abdominal separation; pelvic floor PT can help if present

## Activity Restrictions

### First 6 Weeks — DO NOT:
- ❌ Lift anything heavier than the baby (~7–10 lbs)
- ❌ Drive (until off pain meds + can brake suddenly)
- ❌ Climb stairs excessively (once up/down per day early on is fine)
- ❌ Vigorous exercise, running, jumping
- ❌ Abdominal exercises (crunches, planks, sit-ups)
- ❌ Sex or tampon use
- ❌ Soak in bath/pool/hot tub (shower is fine)
- ❌ Carry heavy laundry baskets, vacuum

### DO:
- ✅ Walk — start short, increase gradually (best thing for recovery)
- ✅ Gentle stretching
- ✅ Pelvic floor exercises (Kegels) — can start within days
- ✅ Deep breathing exercises
- ✅ Rest when possible (hard with a newborn — Jeremy's job to protect this)

## Incision Care

### Daily Care
- Keep clean and dry — gentle soap and water in the shower
- Pat dry (don't rub)
- No creams, ointments, or lotions on the incision unless directed by OB
- Wear loose, high-waisted underwear or pants that don't rub on the incision
- **Don't pick at steri-strips** — they fall off on their own (7–14 days)

### What's Normal
- Pink/red incision line
- Mild bruising around the site
- Itching as it heals (good sign)
- Small amount of clear or slightly yellow drainage
- Numbness around the incision (damaged skin nerves — resolves over months)
- Scar may be raised or lumpy initially

### What's NOT Normal — Contact OB
- Increasing redness, warmth, or swelling at the site
- Pus or foul-smelling drainage
- Incision opens or separates
- Fever ≥ 100.4°F (38°C)
- Increasing pain rather than improving after week 1

## Signs of Complications — When to Call or Go to ER

### Infection (Wound or Uterine)
- Fever ≥ 100.4°F (38°C)
- Foul-smelling vaginal discharge
- Incision: increasing redness, swelling, warmth, drainage
- Worsening pain after the first week

### Blood Clot (DVT / Pulmonary Embolism)
- **Leg:** Pain, swelling, redness, or warmth in one leg (especially calf)
- **Lungs (PE):** Sudden shortness of breath, chest pain, coughing blood — **CALL 911**
- C-section + postpartum = highest risk period for clots
- **Prevention:** Walk early and often; compression stockings if provided; stay hydrated

### Postpartum Hemorrhage
- Soaking a pad in < 1 hour
- Passing clots larger than a golf ball
- Feeling dizzy, lightheaded, or heart racing

### Wound Dehiscence (Incision Opening)
- Incision separates or opens
- Can happen with too much activity too soon
- Cover with clean gauze and call OB immediately

### Postpartum Preeclampsia
- Can occur up to 6 weeks after delivery
- **Signs:** Severe headache, vision changes (blurry, spots), upper right abdominal pain, swelling in face/hands, blood pressure ≥ 140/90
- **This is an emergency** — go to ER

> **Source:** ACOG Practice Bulletin No. 196: "Thromboembolism in Pregnancy," 2018. ACOG FAQ: "C-Section Recovery." UpToDate: "Post-cesarean delivery surgical site infection."

## Emotional Recovery

- **Baby blues** (days 3–14): Mood swings, crying spells, irritability — normal, affects up to 80% of new mothers
- If symptoms persist beyond 2 weeks or worsen → see [[Postpartum Mental Health]]
- C-section recovery can amplify feelings of frustration (can't do things independently) and guilt (sometimes around not having a vaginal birth — both are valid delivery methods)
- **Pain affects mood** — staying on top of pain management helps emotional state
