# Postpartum Mental Health

*For both Jeremy and Lorene. This matters as much as physical health.*

---

## Baby Blues vs. Postpartum Depression

### Baby Blues (Normal)
- **Affects:** Up to 80% of new mothers
- **Onset:** Days 2–5 postpartum
- **Duration:** Resolves within 2 weeks
- **Symptoms:** Mood swings, crying spells, irritability, anxiety, trouble sleeping (beyond the obvious), feeling overwhelmed
- **What helps:** Rest, support, reassurance, time
- **Key distinction:** Symptoms are mild to moderate and self-limiting

### Postpartum Depression (PPD)
- **Affects:** 10–15% of mothers; ~10% of fathers (often underdiagnosed)
- **Onset:** Can start anytime in the first year (most common: weeks 4–6)
- **Duration:** Does NOT resolve on its own — requires treatment
- **Key distinction:** Symptoms are persistent, worsening, and interfere with daily functioning

## Signs of Postpartum Depression (PPD)

### In Lorene (or Any Birthing Parent)
- Persistent sadness, emptiness, or hopelessness lasting > 2 weeks
- Loss of interest or pleasure in activities (including the baby)
- Difficulty bonding with the baby
- Withdrawing from partner, family, or friends
- Changes in appetite (eating much more or much less)
- Sleeping too much or unable to sleep even when baby sleeps
- Overwhelming fatigue or loss of energy beyond normal new-parent tiredness
- Intense irritability, anger, or rage
- Feelings of worthlessness, shame, or guilt ("I'm a bad mother")
- Difficulty concentrating or making decisions
- Crying more than usual or for no apparent reason
- **Thoughts of self-harm or harming the baby** → immediate help needed (see below)

### In Jeremy (Paternal PPD)
Paternal PPD is real and significantly underdiagnosed. Risk increases when the partner has PPD.

- Irritability, anger, or aggression (more common than sadness in men)
- Withdrawing from family or the baby
- Working excessively or finding reasons to stay away
- Increased alcohol, drug use, or risky behavior
- Fatigue, sleep problems
- Feeling disconnected from the baby or partner
- Sadness, hopelessness (may not recognize it as depression)
- Physical symptoms: headaches, digestive issues, weight changes
- Cynicism or indifference that wasn't there before

> **Source:** Paulson JF, Bazemore SD. "Prenatal and Postpartum Depression in Fathers and Its Association With Maternal Depression." *JAMA* 303(19), 2010. Cameron EE et al. "Prevalence of paternal depression in pregnancy and the postpartum: An updated meta-analysis." *Journal of Affective Disorders* 206, 2016.

## Postpartum Anxiety (PPA)

Often co-occurs with or is mistaken for PPD. Can occur independently.

### Signs
- Constant worry that something bad will happen to the baby
- Racing thoughts, especially at night
- Inability to relax or sit still
- Checking on the baby excessively (waking them to confirm breathing)
- Physical symptoms: heart racing, nausea, shortness of breath, dizziness
- Feeling "on edge" constantly
- Difficulty eating or sleeping due to worry
- Avoidance of situations (afraid to be alone with baby, afraid to take baby outside)

### Intrusive Thoughts
- Unwanted, disturbing mental images (baby falling, being hurt, SIDS)
- **These are very common** — up to 90% of new parents have them
- They are NOT the same as wanting to act on them
- **Concerning signs:** Acting on thoughts, making plans, or losing the ability to distinguish thoughts from urges → seek immediate help

## When to Seek Help

### Talk to Your OB or PCP If:
- Baby blues symptoms last beyond 2 weeks
- Symptoms are getting worse, not better
- You can't sleep when the baby sleeps (insomnia beyond opportunity)
- Difficulty caring for yourself or the baby
- Withdrawing from people you love
- Feelings of guilt, worthlessness, or being a bad parent
- Loss of interest in things you normally enjoy

### Call 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) or Go to ER If:
- Thoughts of harming yourself
- Thoughts of harming the baby
- Hearing or seeing things that aren't there
- Feeling disconnected from reality
- Inability to care for the baby
- Panic attacks that don't stop

### Postpartum Support International (PSI)
- **Helpline:** 1-800-944-4773 (call or text)
- **Crisis text:** Text "HELP" to 988
- **Website:** postpartum.net
- **Support groups:** Free, virtual, available in English and Spanish

## Risk Factors

Higher risk for PPD/PPA if:
- History of depression or anxiety (either parent)
- Difficult pregnancy or delivery (**c-section** can be a factor)
- Baby in NICU or health complications
- Lack of social support
- Financial stress
- Relationship difficulties
- Sleep deprivation (cumulative)
- Previous pregnancy loss
- Unplanned pregnancy
- History of trauma

## Support Strategies

### For Lorene
- **Protect her sleep** — Even one 4-hour uninterrupted block makes a measurable difference. Jeremy or Daisy (after March 25) can handle a feeding with expressed milk.
- **Validate feelings** — "You're not crazy. You're not a bad mom. This is hard."
- **Reduce isolation** — Connection matters. Friends, family, support groups.
- **Sunlight and movement** — Even 10 minutes outside improves mood
- **Nutrition** — Regular meals, hydration (especially while breastfeeding)
- **Lower the bar** — The house doesn't need to be clean. Survival mode is OK.
- **Screen at 6-week OB visit** — Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) is standard; ask for it if not offered

### For Jeremy
- **Acknowledge it's hard for you too** — Being the sole income + sole physical support + sleep deprivation + not getting paternity leave is objectively difficult
- **Don't compare** — "She had surgery, I shouldn't complain" is a trap. Your struggles are also valid.
- **Talk to someone** — Friend (Dan?), therapist, PSI helpline. Paternal PPD screening exists.
- **Watch your coping mechanisms** — Increased drinking, overworking, numbing with screens are flags
- **You don't have to have it all together** — Asking for help is a strength

### As a Couple
- **Communicate proactively** — Check in daily, even briefly ("How are you really?")
- **Be a team, not scorekeepers** — "I did more diapers" leads nowhere
- **Expect relationship strain** — It's normal. A new baby is the biggest stressor a couple can face.
- **Physical affection without pressure** — Holding hands, hugging, presence
- **Couples therapy** is not a sign of failure — it's maintenance
- **Remember:** You're both learning. No one is born knowing how to do this.

## Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS)

A validated 10-question screening tool. Score ≥ 13 indicates likely depression. Available for both mothers and fathers.

Ask your OB/PCP to administer it at:
- 2-week postpartum check
- 6-week postpartum check
- Any visit where you're struggling

It's also available online (free): search "EPDS screening tool" — but professional evaluation is always better than self-scoring.

> **Source:** Cox JL et al. "Detection of Postnatal Depression: Development of the 10-item Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale." *British Journal of Psychiatry* 150, 1987. ACOG Committee Opinion No. 757: "Screening for Perinatal Depression," 2018.
