## Circadian Rhythm Development

Newborns don't have a circadian rhythm — they develop one over the first 3–4 months.

### Timeline
| Age | Sleep Pattern |
|-----|--------------|
| 0–6 weeks | No circadian rhythm. Sleep is distributed evenly across day and night. Driven by ultradian cycles (~50 min). |
| 6–8 weeks | Melatonin production begins. Longest sleep stretch starts shifting to nighttime. |
| 8–12 weeks | Cortisol rhythm develops. More predictable nap times emerge. |
| 3–4 months | True circadian rhythm established. Sleep architecture matures (sleep cycles lengthen to ~90 min). |

### What Helps Circadian Development
1. **Light exposure** — Bright daylight during wake times; dim warm light in evenings
2. **Dark at night** — Blackout curtains, no screens, minimal light for nighttime feeds
3. **Consistent bedtime routine** — Same cues every night (Lucas & Georgia's bath→massage→feed→sleep is ideal)
4. **Social cues** — Active, engaging during day; quiet, boring at night

> Lucas & Georgia's 6:30 PM bedtime aligns with the melatonin production window — this is well-supported by the research. Their use of warm yellow light in the evening and blackout curtains is exactly what sleep scientists recommend.

> **Source:** Rivkees SA. "Developing Circadian Rhythmicity in Infants." *Pediatrics* 112(2), 2003. Also: Mindell JA et al. "A Nightly Bedtime Routine: Impact on Sleep in Young Children." *Sleep* 32(5), 2009.

## Wake Windows by Age

Wake windows = the time baby can comfortably stay awake between sleeps. Exceeding these leads to overtiredness, which paradoxically makes sleep harder.

| Age | Wake Window | Naps/Day | Total Day Sleep | Night Sleep |
|-----|------------|----------|----------------|-------------|
| 0–4 weeks | 35–60 min | 5–8 | 8–9 hrs | 8–9 hrs (fragmented) |
| 4–8 weeks | 45–75 min | 4–6 | 6–8 hrs | 8–10 hrs |
| 2–3 months | 60–90 min | 4–5 | 5–7 hrs | 9–11 hrs |
| 3–4 months | 75–120 min | 3–4 | 4–5 hrs | 10–12 hrs |
| 5–6 months | 2–2.5 hrs | 3 | 3–4 hrs | 10–12 hrs |
| 7–9 months | 2.5–3.5 hrs | 2–3 | 2.5–3.5 hrs | 10–12 hrs |
| 10–12 months | 3–4 hrs | 2 | 2–3 hrs | 10–12 hrs |

### Sleepy Cues
- **Early:** Staring into space, slower movements, less engaged
- **Clear:** Yawning, rubbing eyes/ears, fussiness
- **Late:** Arching back, crying, jerky movements (overtired — harder to settle)

> **Theo at Day 7:** His wake windows are ~35–60 minutes. Watch for the early cues — by the time he's yawning, the window is nearly closed.

> **Source:** Galland BC et al. "Normal Sleep Patterns in Infants and Children: A Systematic Review." *Sleep Medicine Reviews* 16(3), 2012.

## Sleep Regressions

Sleep regressions are periods when a baby who was sleeping well suddenly isn't. They're tied to developmental leaps and changes in sleep architecture.

### The Major Regressions

#### 4-Month Regression (Most Significant)
- **What happens:** Sleep architecture permanently matures from newborn pattern (2 stages) to adult-like pattern (4 stages). Baby now experiences light sleep cycles and wakes more between them.
- **Duration:** 2–6 weeks
- **Signs:** Waking every 1–2 hours, fighting naps, shorter naps, fussier
- **What helps:** This isn't temporary — it's a permanent change. Now is when consistent sleep associations matter most. If rocking/feeding to sleep becomes unsustainable, this is the natural inflection point for sleep training.
- **Timing:** Usually 3.5–4.5 months (adjusted age)

#### 8-Month Regression
- **What happens:** Separation anxiety peaks + crawling/pulling up milestones
- **Duration:** 2–3 weeks
- **Signs:** Crying when put down, standing in crib, resisting naps
- **What helps:** Extra reassurance during day, consistent bedtime routine, practice new skills during daytime

#### 12-Month Regression
- **What happens:** Walking milestone + nap transition (2→1 nap begins)
- **Duration:** 1–3 weeks
- **Signs:** Refusing second nap, bedtime resistance
- **What helps:** Don't drop to one nap yet (most babies aren't ready until 13–15 months); ride it out

### Other Disruptions (Not True Regressions)
- **6 weeks:** Growth spurt, increased night waking
- **6 months:** Teething begins, sitting milestone
- **10 months:** Cruising, language development
- **18 months:** Separation anxiety second peak, toddler boundary testing

> **Source:** Mindell JA, Owens JA. *A Clinical Guide to Pediatric Sleep*, 3rd Edition, 2015.

## Evidence-Based Sleep Training Approaches

**Not recommended before 4 months.** Before then, focus on healthy sleep habits and associations. These are for when Jeremy and Lorene decide they're ready.

### Methods (From Gentlest to Most Direct)

#### 1. Fading / Gradual Withdrawal (FIO — Fading of Intervention)
- Slowly reduce your involvement over days/weeks
- E.g., rock to drowsy → hand on chest → sit in room → leave room
- **Pros:** Very gentle, minimal crying
- **Cons:** Slow (can take 2–4 weeks), requires patience and consistency
- **Best for:** Parents uncomfortable with any crying

#### 2. Chair Method (Sleep Lady Shuffle)
- Sit in a chair next to the crib; move the chair farther away every 2–3 nights
- Can verbally and physically comfort but don't pick up
- **Pros:** Parent presence is reassuring
- **Cons:** Can be overstimulating for some babies; takes 2–3 weeks

#### 3. Ferber Method (Graduated Extinction / "Check and Console")
- Put baby down awake; leave the room; return at increasing intervals (3 min, 5 min, 10 min)
- Brief check: reassure verbally, pat, leave. Don't pick up.
- **Pros:** Well-studied, typically works in 3–7 nights
- **Cons:** Involves crying; emotionally hard for parents
- **Evidence:** Ferber's original research + Mindell 2006 meta-analysis of 52 studies confirming efficacy with no adverse effects

#### 4. Full Extinction ("Cry It Out")
- Put baby down awake, leave, don't return until morning feed time
- **Pros:** Fastest results (often 3–5 nights)
- **Cons:** Most crying; hardest on parents; often misunderstood/stigmatized
- **Evidence:** Gradisar et al. (2016) in *Pediatrics* — 5-year follow-up found no difference in emotional/behavioral problems, parent-child attachment, or cortisol levels between CIO group and control

### Key Principles (All Methods)
- **Consistency is more important than the method chosen**
- Both parents must agree and commit
- Pick a start date with no travel/disruptions for 2 weeks
- Good sleep hygiene first: dark room, white noise, consistent routine, age-appropriate schedule
- Feed before routine (not as last step — avoid feed-to-sleep association)

> **Source:** Gradisar M et al. "Behavioral Interventions for Infant Sleep Problems: A Randomized Controlled Trial." *Pediatrics* 137(6), 2016. Also: Mindell JA et al. "Behavioral Treatment of Bedtime Problems and Night Wakings in Infants and Young Children." *Sleep* 29(10), 2006.
