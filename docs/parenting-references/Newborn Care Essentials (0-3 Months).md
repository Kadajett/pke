## Safe Sleep (AAP 2022 Guidelines)

The AAP updated safe sleep recommendations in June 2022. These are non-negotiable:

### The ABCs of Safe Sleep
- **A**lone — no co-sleeping on adult beds, couches, or armchairs
- **B**ack — always place baby on their back for every sleep
- **C**rib — firm, flat surface with a fitted sheet, nothing else

### Specifics
- **Firm, flat surface** — mattress should not indent when baby is placed on it
- **No loose bedding** — no blankets, pillows, bumper pads, stuffed animals, or positioners
- **Room-sharing without bed-sharing** — recommended for at least the first 6 months (reduces SIDS risk by up to 50%)
- **Swaddling** — acceptable until baby shows signs of rolling (usually ~2–4 months), then transition to a sleep sack
- **Pacifier at sleep time** — associated with reduced SIDS risk; offer after breastfeeding is established (~3–4 weeks)
- **Room temperature** — 68–72°F (20–22°C); dress baby in one layer more than you'd wear
- **No inclined sleepers** — anything over 10° incline is unsafe (Fisher-Price recall, 2019)

> **Source:** AAP Policy Statement: "Sleep-Related Infant Deaths: Updated 2022 Recommendations for Reducing Infant Deaths in the Sleep Environment." *Pediatrics* 150(1), 2022.

## Feeding Schedules and Cues

### Hunger Cues (in order of escalation)
1. **Early:** Lip smacking, rooting, hands to mouth
2. **Active:** Fidgeting, fussing, head turning
3. **Late:** Crying — try to feed before this stage; crying is a late cue and makes latching harder

### Breastfeeding
- **Frequency:** 8–12 times per 24 hours in the first month
- **Duration:** 10–20 minutes per breast; let baby finish one side before offering the other
- **On demand** — not on a schedule; newborns cluster feed, especially in evenings
- **Growth spurts:** Expect increased demand around days 7–10, weeks 2–3, and week 6

### Bottle Feeding (formula or expressed milk)
- **Newborn:** 1–2 oz per feeding, every 2–3 hours
- **1 month:** 3–4 oz per feeding, every 3–4 hours
- **2–3 months:** 4–5 oz per feeding, every 3–4 hours
- **Paced bottle feeding** — hold bottle horizontal, let baby control pace; prevents overfeeding and supports breastfeeding if doing both

> **Note for Theo:** Lorene is breastfeeding. Jeremy can support with burping, skin-to-skin, and paced bottle feeding when expressed milk is available. See also [[Tips - Sleep Routine (Lucas & Georgia)]] for the feeding→sleep routine.

### Burping
- Burp midway through and after every feeding
- Three positions: over shoulder, sitting upright on lap, face-down on lap
- If no burp after 2–3 minutes, it's fine — move on

## Diaper Output — What's Normal

This is the primary way to know if baby is getting enough milk in the early days.

### By Day (first week)
| Day | Wet Diapers | Stool Diapers | Stool Color |
|-----|------------|---------------|-------------|
| 1 | 1 | 1–2 | Black/tarry (meconium) |
| 2 | 2 | 1–2 | Dark green/black |
| 3 | 3 | 2–3 | Greenish-brown (transitional) |
| 4 | 4 | 3+ | Yellow, seedy |
| 5–7 | 6+ | 3–4+ | Yellow, seedy, loose |

### After First Week
- **Wet:** 6–8+ heavy wet diapers per day
- **Stool:** Breastfed babies may poop every feeding or go up to 7–10 days without (after ~6 weeks) — both are normal
- **Formula-fed:** More formed, tan/brown, typically 1–3x per day

### Red Flags
- Fewer than 6 wet diapers/day after day 4
- No stool for 24+ hours in the first week
- Brick dust (orange/red urate crystals) after day 3 — may indicate dehydration
- White, red, or black stool after meconium has passed

> **Source:** AAP *Caring for Your Baby and Young Child: Birth to Age 5*, 7th Edition, 2019.

## Warning Signs — When to Call the Pediatrician

### Call Immediately (or go to ER)
- **Fever ≥ 100.4°F (38°C) rectally** in a baby under 3 months — this is an emergency, no exceptions
- Difficulty breathing: grunting, flaring nostrils, retractions (ribs visible with each breath), blue lips/face
- Inconsolable crying for 2+ hours
- Extreme lethargy — hard to wake, floppy, unresponsive
- Seizures (rhythmic jerking, eye rolling, stiffening)
- Projectile vomiting (not spit-up — forceful, shoots across the room)
- Bulging or sunken fontanelle (soft spot)
- Signs of dehydration: no tears, dry mouth, no wet diaper in 6+ hours

### Call During Office Hours
- Yellow skin/eyes worsening after day 5 (jaundice — see [[2026-03-13 - Day 6]] for Theo's notes)
- Umbilical cord area: redness spreading, pus, foul smell
- Persistent diaper rash not improving with barrier cream
- Congestion making feeding difficult
- Eye discharge (sticky, yellow/green)
- Not gaining weight or losing weight after day 5

## Umbilical Cord Care

- **Keep dry** — fold diaper below the stump
- **No submersion baths** until stump falls off (sponge baths only) — usually 1–3 weeks
- **Normal:** May look dark, dried, slightly smelly near the end
- **Abnormal:** Active bleeding, pus, redness on surrounding skin, foul smell

## Vitamin D Supplementation

- **400 IU/day** starting within the first few days of life for all breastfed or partially breastfed infants
- Continue until baby takes 32 oz/day of vitamin D-fortified formula or milk (after age 1)

> **Source:** AAP Committee on Nutrition. "Prevention of Rickets and Vitamin D Deficiency in Infants, Children, and Adolescents." *Pediatrics* 122(5), 2008.

## Skin-to-Skin Contact

- Regulates baby's temperature, heart rate, breathing, and blood sugar
- Promotes bonding and breastfeeding
- **Both parents benefit** — Jeremy doing skin-to-skin is equally important
- Recommended: at least 1 hour daily in the first months
